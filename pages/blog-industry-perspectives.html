Some Industry Perspectives: Skills Companies Want

By Faza Naziila Dias Shanti | Published on March 21, 2025

Companies today are not only looking for people with hard skills (technical skills), but also soft skills that support the job. The condition also implements to UI/UX designer who have to always keep an eye to user's need.

UI designer must have to utilize user's interaction with the product that also known as interface. However, UX designer task is to build a user-friendly product, service, and technology. The unity of the tasks makes UI/UX designer to produce an application that is not only good to look at visually, but also responsive and easy to use. In that case, UI/UX designer also need to have both hard skill and soft skill to make top quality products and be an expert in their career.

Hard Skills for UI/UX Designers

Hard skills of UI/UX designer are specifically technical, such as:

  • Design Skills: UI/UX designer must have the proficiency in design tools like Figma, Adobe XD, Sketch, or InVision.
  • Visual Design & UI: an UI/UX designer must have a deep understanding of visual design principles, including typography, visual hierarchy, colour & contrast, layout & grid systems.
  • User Research & Analysis: to understand user needs, behaviours, and problems this skill is needed. UI/UX designer have to be able to conduct user interview to gain insights into user experiences, utilize surveys and questionnaires to gather quantitative data, analyses user behaviour data, and creates user personas to understand the target audience better.
  • Information Architecture: this skill involves structuring information with the intention to make users can easily navigate our products. It is needed to understand card storing, user flow & navigation, and hierarchy of information. Card storing is a technique to group information according to user expectations. User flow & navigation is a skill of designing intuitive navigation paths to guide users seamlessly. Lastly, we have to understand the prioritize important information for better usability.
  • Wireframing & Prototyping: Before finalizing the design, UI/UX designers create wireframes and prototypes to test their ideas. There are kinds of wireframes and prototypes to reach difference need. Low-fidelity wireframes are rough sketches that outline the basic layout. High-fidelity wireframes is detailed designs that reflect the final product. Also, Interactive prototypes is for simulated designs that can be tested by users before development.
  • UX Writing: is the skill to be able to write clear and effective text within a digital interface. in UX writing there are micro copy, a short text in buttons or notifications that help users understand their function, error messages, an informative error messages that help users resolve issues, and Call-to-Action (CTA), that is a persuasive message that encourage users to act, such as "Start Now" or "Sign Up for Free."
  • Understanding of HTML & CSS: Although coding is not always required, knowledge of HTML and CSS can help designer to communicate effectively with developers. It also helps designer to adjust designs according to technical limitations. And it benefits to can test small design changes directly in a browser.

Soft Skills for UI/UX Designers

There are also soft skills, interpersonal and mental skill, that help UI/UX designers work effectively in teams and understand user needs. The soft skills are:

  • Communication Skills: Effective communication is essential in any workplace, whether it's verbal, written, or digital. Employees who can clearly convey ideas, actively listen, and collaborate effectively are highly valued by companies.
  • Empathy: it is a core of UX design. Designer must be able to view from the user's perspective to understand their challenges. By doing so, designer then can create solution that align with user needs and constraints. It also helps to avoid personal assumptions and rely on data for design decisions.
  • Leadership: Leadership is not just about managing people; it's about taking initiative, making decisions, and inspiring others to work towards common goals. Even non-managerial employees benefit from leadership skills by demonstrating responsibility and problem-solving abilities.
  • Teamwork: The ability to work well with others is crucial in modern workplaces, where collaboration drives innovation and efficiency. Employees who can adapt to diverse teams, contribute ideas, and resolve conflicts constructively are assets to any company.
  • Problem-Solving Skills: Employers seek individuals who can analyze challenges, think critically, and develop effective solutions. The ability to handle unexpected situations and find creative approaches to problems makes an employee indispensable.
  • Time Management: Efficiently managing tasks, meeting deadlines, and prioritizing responsibilities are key to productivity. Employees with strong time management skills can balance multiple tasks without compromising quality, leading to better performance and reduced stress.

Key Takeaway: Self-development should focus on both hard skills (technical expertise) and soft skills (interpersonal and organizational abilities) to ensure long-term career success.
